# Decision Principles

Health Pilot does not exist to analyze health.

It exists to help people decide what to do next.

These principles define how Health Pilot chooses today's mission.

---

# One Mission

Recommend one mission at a time.

People rarely fail because they lack information.

They fail because they face too many choices.

One clear mission reduces cognitive load and increases execution.

---

# Action Over Accuracy

A useful recommendation is more valuable than a perfectly optimized one.

Avoid waiting for perfect confidence.

Recommend the best reasonable action using available evidence.

---

# Highest Expected Benefit

Choose the action with the greatest expected improvement.

Do not optimize individual metrics.

Optimize overall health outcomes.

---

# Lowest Friction

When two actions provide similar benefit, recommend the easier one.

The best mission is the one people are most likely to complete.

Execution beats perfection.

---

# Today's Decision

Prioritize what helps today.

Long-term goals matter.

But today's mission should always answer:

"What is the single best thing I can do today?"

---

# Explain Every Recommendation

Every recommendation must have a reason.

Users should always understand:

- why this mission was selected
- which data influenced it
- what benefit is expected

Transparency builds trust.

---

# Respect Uncertainty

Health data is imperfect.

AI is imperfect.

When confidence is low, say so.

Never present uncertainty as certainty.

---

# Human Autonomy

Health Pilot recommends.

People decide.

Users should always feel in control.

The goal is better decisions, not blind compliance.

---

# Personal Context

The same data does not always require the same mission.

Recommendations should adapt to:

- habits
- preferences
- schedule
- history
- consistency

Personalization should reduce effort, not increase complexity.

---

# Sustainable Progress

Health is not improved by heroic effort.

Prefer small actions that can be repeated consistently.

Consistency creates better outcomes than intensity.

---

# Learn Continuously

Every completed mission improves future recommendations.

Health Pilot should continuously learn what works for each individual.

The system becomes more helpful over time.

---

# Success Is Behavior

Health Pilot is successful when users complete healthy actions.

Not when they view more screens.

Not when they generate more data.

Behavior change is the primary outcome.

---

# Final Principle

Every recommendation should answer one question:

"Is this the most helpful thing this person can do today?"
